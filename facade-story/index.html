About the Project

This research develops a computational framework for detecting, classifying, and evaluating green infrastructure (GI) installed on building façades at the urban scale. Rather than relying on manual surveys or coarse satellite imagery, the pipeline fuses street-level photogrammetry, semantic segmentation, and 3-D building geometry to produce façade-resolved GI inventories.

Outcomes inform urban heat island mitigation strategies, biodiversity corridors, stormwater management planning, and energy-efficiency retrofits — connecting building-skin vegetation directly to city-wide environmental targets.

The methodology is replicable across any city where point-cloud data or photographic surveys are available, and all core tools are released as open-source software.

Methodology Pipeline

1

Data Acquisition

Street-level panoramic imagery collected alongside building footprints and height data from open municipal datasets.

OSM · LiDAR · GSV
2

Façade Segmentation

Deep-learning semantic segmentation isolates façade planes, windows, doors, and vegetation regions per image.

CNN · DeepLabV3+
3

3-D Projection

Detected GI regions back-projected onto georeferenced 3-D building meshes to compute real-world coverage areas.

Raycasting · PCL
4

Typology Classification

Six GI typologies assigned via a rule-based classifier combining visual texture features and geometry context.

RF Classifier
5

Performance Scoring

Each façade segment scored against thermal regulation, biodiversity potential, and stormwater indices.

Multi-criteria
6

Urban Aggregation

Façade-level results aggregated to block and district scales to support planning and policy decisions.

Results

Façade Green Infrastructure Coverage

Across the 1,240 façades surveyed, 22.4 % carry some form of green infrastructure. Coverage density peaks on south- and east-facing orientations and is strongly correlated with pre-1960 building stock. Street-facing frontages account for 68 % of total detected GI area.

South-facing: 31 % East-facing: 27 % West-facing: 19 % North-facing: 8 % Mean coverage: 22.4 %

Green Infrastructure Typology Distribution

Six distinct typologies were identified. Climbing plants on masonry represent the most prevalent system (41 %), followed by pot/planter arrangements (24 %) and modular living wall panels (18 %). Purpose-built green façade systems remain rare (< 5 %).

Climbing plants: 41 % Planters/pots: 24 % Living wall modules: 18 % Trellis systems: 12 % Pergola/canopy: 5 % Engineered GF: 4 %

Environmental Performance Scores

Multi-criteria scoring reveals that modular living wall systems consistently outperform opportunistic climbing vegetation on thermal and stormwater metrics, despite their lower prevalence. Biodiversity indices favour climbing species with complex canopy structure.

Thermal score avg: 0.61 Biodiversity avg: 0.54 Stormwater avg: 0.43 Aesthetic avg: 0.72

Accuracy & Validation

Manual ground-truth surveys on a stratified sample of 120 façades confirm an overall detection accuracy of 91.3 % (F1), with typology classification reaching 87.6 % on the six-class task. False positives are predominantly caused by potted trees at ground level misidentified as wall-mounted GI.

Detection F1: 91.3 % Classification F1: 87.6 % IoU: 0.84 Sample n=120

Key Findings

🌡️

Thermal Buffering

Façades with > 40 % GI coverage exhibit surface temperature reductions of 3–6 °C during summer peak hours compared to bare masonry.

🐝

Biodiversity Corridors

Contiguous GI-covered façades along street networks form potential ecological stepping stones connecting park habitats up to 2.3 km apart.

💧

Stormwater Retention

Living wall systems retain an estimated 28–55 L/m² per rainfall event, meaningfully reducing peak runoff in dense urban blocks.

⚡

Energy Implications

Thermal buffering data extrapolated to annual cooling loads suggests 7–12 % reduction potential for GI-dense façades in temperate climates.

📍

Spatial Inequality

GI coverage is 3× higher in affluent residential neighbourhoods vs. commercial/industrial zones, highlighting equity gaps in urban greening.

🔧

Retrofit Potential

Over 60 % of GI-free façades meet structural and orientation criteria for low-intervention greening with climbing plant support systems.

Frequently Asked Questions

Satellite and aerial imagery capture horizontal green surfaces (roofs, parks, tree canopies) well, but vertical surfaces — building façades — are largely invisible from above. This framework operates on street-level and oblique imagery combined with 3-D building geometry, giving façade-resolved measurements that are simply not accessible from overhead data sources.
The core pipeline requires street-level imagery (Google Street View, Mapillary, or custom surveys), building footprints (available from OpenStreetMap for most major cities), and optional height data. The deep-learning segmentation model was trained on diverse urban scenes and generalises well across European and North American building stock without retraining.
Yes. The segmentation model weights, geometry projection scripts, and performance scoring framework are released under MIT licence. Derived spatial datasets for the study area are released under ODbL. Raw street-view imagery remains subject to provider licensing terms.
The typology schema was derived from a literature review of European green façade standards (including BS 8545, EFB guidelines, and LEED v4 criteria) combined with an inductive classification of 3,000 manually annotated façade images. The six classes balance operational distinctiveness with detection feasibility given available image resolution.
Known limitations include: (1) temporal snapshot — street imagery captures GI state at one point in time, missing seasonal variation; (2) occlusion — parked vehicles and street furniture obstruct up to 18 % of façade area; (3) imagery resolution constrains detection of small-scale features such as individual planter pots below ~20 cm. Future work will address seasonal multi-temporal analysis and drone-based close-range data collection.
